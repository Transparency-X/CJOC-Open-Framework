# Task 1.2.2: Briefing Paper for Oireachtas Joint Committee on Justice

## THE SILOED OVERSIGHT GAP: WHY THE CJOC IS NECESSARY NOW

### Executive Summary for Committee Members
Ireland’s criminal justice system is monitored by competent but fragmented oversight bodies. This structure creates **systemic blind spots at agency intersections** that no existing body can investigate. The CJOC fills this gap without replacing or undermining existing institutions. It is also a mandatory requirement under the EU Anti-Corruption Directive, with non-compliance exposing the State to infringement proceedings.

---

### 1. The Structural Problem: Siloed Oversight in Practice

#### Current Architecture
| Body | Mandate | Jurisdictional Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Fiosrú | Individual Garda complaints | Cannot investigate prison, probation, or court admin failures |
| Inspector of Prisons | Prison conditions & custody deaths | Cannot investigate Garda handover failures or probation release protocols |
| Judicial Council | Judicial conduct & sentencing | Cannot investigate Courts Service administration or DPP file delays |
| Ombudsman | Administrative maladministration | Limited jurisdiction over security-sensitive justice agencies |
| SIPO | Elected officials & senior office holders | Does not cover operational justice system integrity |

#### Documented Cross-Agency Failures (Anonymised Case Studies)
1.  **Evidence Chain Breakdown:** Forensic samples lost during transfer between AGS and FSI. Neither Fiosrú nor any inspectorate had jurisdiction over the *inter-agency protocol*. Internal reviews by both agencies blamed each other. No independent cross-cutting investigation occurred.
2.  **Prisoner Release Information Gap:** Probation Service risk assessment not communicated to IPS due to incompatible IT systems. Prisoner released without appropriate supervision conditions. Inspector of Prisons could only review IPS records; Probation Service’s failure was outside remit.
3.  **Victim Communication Failure:** DPP administrative delay in notifying victims of case outcomes. Courts Service and DPP each cited resource constraints. No body had mandate to audit the *end-to-end victim communication pathway* across both agencies.

> **Key Insight for Committee:** Each individual agency may be operating within its own standards. The failure occurs in the *space between agencies*. No current body occupies that space.

### 2. International Compliance Imperative

#### EU Anti-Corruption Directive (2024/...)
| Directive Requirement | Current Irish Status | CJOC Solution |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Independent national anti-corruption oversight body covering justice sector | ❌ Fragmented across multiple bodies | ✅ Single statutory body with cross-agency mandate |
| Proactive systemic risk assessment methodology | ❌ Reactive, complaint-driven only | ✅ Mandatory thematic audits + data analytics dashboard |
| National coordination mechanism for anti-corruption efforts | ❌ Ad-hoc inter-departmental liaison | ✅ Statutory interoperability framework + MoUs |
| Regular public reporting on justice sector integrity | ❌ Disparate annual reports, no unified metrics | ✅ Annual "State of Criminal Justice Integrity" report |

#### GRECO Evaluation Cycle V Recommendations
-   **Recommendation vii:** "Strengthen proactive integrity monitoring mechanisms across law enforcement and penitentiary institutions." → **CJOC Heads 15–16**
-   **Recommendation ix:** "Establish coordinated data collection on corruption risks in the justice sector." → **CJOC Head 17 + Schedule 1**
-   **Recommendation xii:** "Ensure independent oversight bodies have adequate investigative powers." → **CJOC Heads 21–24**

> ⚠️ **Compliance Risk:** Ireland’s next GRECO evaluation is scheduled for Q3 2027. Without enacted CJOC legislation, Ireland faces probable non-compliance findings and potential EU infringement proceedings.

### 3. What the CJOC Does NOT Do (Constitutional Safeguards)

| Misconception | Reality | Legal Basis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| "CJOC will second-guess judges" | ❌ Explicitly prohibited from reviewing any judicial decision or case management order | Head 19(1)(a) |
| "CJOC will direct prosecutions" | ❌ Explicitly prohibited from reviewing DPP charging or prosecution decisions | Head 19(1)(b) |
| "CJOC replaces Fiosrú" | ❌ Individual complaints remain exclusively with Fiosrú; CJOC refers individual misconduct back | Head 19(1)(d) + Head 25 |
| "CJOC regulates lawyers" | ❌ LSRA retains exclusive jurisdiction over legal profession | Head 19(1)(c) |
| "CJOC is controlled by the Minister" | ❌ Statutory independence + separate Central Fund Vote + Oireachtas-led appointments | Heads 5, 9, 33 |

---

### 4. Priority Questions for Committee Consideration

1.  Are the jurisdictional exclusions in Head 19 sufficiently precise to survive constitutional challenge while still enabling meaningful cross-agency oversight?
2.  Should the Committee recommend strengthening or narrowing the investigative powers in Heads 21–24?
3.  Is the proposed board composition (Head 8) appropriately balanced between legal, technical, and operational expertise?
4.  Should the Bill include a statutory review clause requiring independent evaluation of the CJOC’s effectiveness after 3 years?
5.  Are additional safeguards needed for whistleblower protections beyond those in Head 35?
