## MEMORANDUM FOR THE GOVERNMENT

### 1. Purpose of the General Scheme
The purpose of this General Scheme is to obtain Government approval for the drafting of the Criminal Justice Oversight Commission Bill 2026. This Bill establishes an independent statutory body to provide proactive, systemic oversight of integrity risks across Ireland’s criminal justice sector, addressing a critical structural gap identified by the Council of Europe’s GRECO and required under the EU Anti-Corruption Directive (2024/...).

### 2. Policy Context and Rationale
Ireland’s current criminal justice oversight architecture is fragmented across siloed bodies (Fiosrú, Inspector of Prisons, Judicial Council). While effective within their individual remits, no single entity has jurisdiction to investigate cross-agency integrity failures or monitor systemic health proactively. The proposed CJOC fills this gap without encroaching on constitutional protections for judicial independence or prosecutorial discretion.

### 3. Key Provisions
-   Establishes the CJOC as an independent statutory body with ring-fenced funding.
-   Grants powers of entry, document compulsion, and staff examination modelled on HIQA.
-   Creates a binding Integrity Directive mechanism for systemic remediation.
-   Explicitly excludes judicial decision-making and DPP prosecutorial discretion from jurisdiction.
-   Mandates cross-agency data sharing with GDPR-compliant safeguards.
-   Designates the CJOC as the national competent authority for EU/GRECO justice-sector reporting.

### 4. Financial Implications
-   Year 1 establishment cost: €3.2M (separate Central Fund Vote).
-   Steady-state annual operating cost: €4.8M–€6.2M.
-   Compliance costs for in-scope agencies estimated at €250K–€750K capital + 1.5–3.0 FTE ongoing per agency.
-   Full Regulatory Impact Assessment completed and appended.

### 5. Legal and Constitutional Advice
The Attorney General has advised that the jurisdictional exclusions in Head 19 are sufficient to protect Articles 30.3 and 34–35 of the Constitution. The Office of the Parliamentary Counsel has confirmed the Scheme is draftable.

### 6. Recommendation
It is recommended that the Government approve this General Scheme and refer it to the Oireachtas Joint Committee on Justice for Pre-Legislative Scrutiny.

## DETAILED HEADS

### PART 1: PRELIMINARY AND GENERAL

#### Head 1: Short title and commencement
Provides that the Act may be cited as the Criminal Justice Oversight Commission Act 2026 and shall come into operation on such day or days as the Minister for Justice may appoint by order.

#### Head 2: Interpretation
Defines key terms including “Commission,” “cross-agency integrity risk,” “in-scope agency,” “systemic integrity,” “Integrity Directive,” and “authorised officer.” Incorporates definitions by reference to the Data Protection Act 2018 and Protected Disclosures Act 2014.

#### Head 3: Establishment day
Empowers the Minister to appoint the establishment day by order.

---

### PART 2: ESTABLISHMENT AND INDEPENDENCE

#### Head 4: Establishment of the Commission
Establishes the Criminal Justice Oversight Commission as a body corporate with perpetual succession, capable of suing and being sued, acquiring and disposing of property, and entering contracts.

#### Head 5: Independence of the Commission
Provides that the Commission shall be independent in the performance of its functions and shall not be subject to direction, control, or supervision by any Minister, Government department, or in-scope agency in its monitoring, auditing, investigative, or reporting functions.  
*Note: Modelled on s.10 of the Electoral Reform Act 2022.*

#### Head 6: Seal of the Commission
Provides for the authentication of the Commission’s seal by signature of the Chairperson or authorised member.

#### Head 7: Principal functions
Sets out the seven principal functions: systemic monitoring; thematic audits; cross-agency investigations; issuing Integrity Directives; publishing data and reports; serving as national competent authority for EU/GRECO reporting; promoting interoperability between oversight bodies.

---

### PART 3: MEMBERSHIP AND GOVERNANCE

#### Head 8: Membership
Provides for 5 members, one appointed as Chairperson. Requires collective expertise in law/constitutional governance, data science/forensic analytics, human rights/criminology, public finance/auditing, and senior public administration.

#### Head 9: Appointment process
(1) Members appointed by President on advice of Government.  
(2) Government shall only advise appointment of persons recommended by a cross-party Oireachtas committee.  
(3) Selection criteria and process to be published in advance.  
*Note: Insulates appointments from executive capture; modelled on Electoral Commission precedent.*

#### Head 10: Term of office
5-year term, eligible for one reappointment. Staggered initial terms to ensure continuity.

#### Head 11: Removal from office
Removal only by President on advice of Government following resolutions passed by both Houses of the Oireachtas on stated grounds of incapacity, misbehaviour, or conflict of interest.

#### Head 12: Chief Executive Officer
CEO appointed through open competition administered by Public Appointments Service. Responsible for day-to-day management, administration, and staffing. Accountable to the Commission, not to any Minister.

#### Head 13: Staff
Commission may appoint employees with consent of Minister for Public Expenditure, NDP Delivery and Reform. Staff are civil servants assigned to the Commission.

#### Head 14: Committees
Commission may establish committees including Data Governance, Audit Standards, and International Compliance committees. May include external experts in non-voting advisory capacity.

---

### PART 4: SYSTEMIC MONITORING AND AUDIT

#### Head 15: Systemic integrity monitoring
Requires continuous monitoring of systemic integrity across all in-scope agencies through analysis of administrative data, complaint trends, parallel oversight body reports, and other indicators.

#### Head 16: Thematic audits
(1) Commission may conduct thematic audits at own initiative or upon Oireachtas committee request.  
(2) Audits may examine policies, SOPs, resource allocation, training, IT systems, and cultural indicators.  
(3) Minimum of two thematic audits per calendar year required.

#### Head 17: Data collection framework
(1) In-scope agencies must provide data reasonably required by Commission.  
(2) Commission shall develop standardised data schemas.  
(3) Data provision obligations subject to Data Protection Act 2018 and DPIA requirements.  
(4) Agencies must adapt systems to comply within 18 months of establishment day.

#### Head 18: Publication of findings
Commission shall publish monitoring and audit findings subject to redaction only for national security, ongoing investigations, or personal data protection. Presumption of maximum transparency.

---

### PART 5: INVESTIGATIVE POWERS AND JURISDICTION

#### Head 19: Jurisdictional exclusions
(1) Commission shall NOT have jurisdiction to:  
  (a) Review judicial decisions, rulings, sentencing, or case management orders;  
  (b) Review DPP decisions to initiate, continue, or discontinue prosecutions;  
  (c) Regulate or discipline solicitors or barristers;  
  (d) Adjudicate individual misconduct complaints within another body’s remit.  
(2) Where evidence of excluded matters arises, Commission shall cease inquiry into that matter, refer to appropriate body, and may continue only regarding systemic/administrative enablers.  
*Note: Core constitutional safeguard. Drafted with AGO input.*

#### Head 20: Cross-agency jurisdiction
Commission has jurisdiction to investigate any cross-agency integrity risk involving two or more in-scope agencies, notwithstanding that elements may fall within another body’s remit.

#### Head 21: Power to enter premises
Authorised officers may enter any premises of an in-scope agency at reasonable times without prior notice for audit or investigation purposes.  
*Note: Modelled on s.8 Health Act 2007 (HIQA).*

#### Head 22: Power to compel documents and data
Authorised officers may require production of any document, record, or electronic data relevant to audit/investigation; may take copies, extracts, or temporary possession.

#### Head 23: Power to compel attendance
Authorised officers may require employees, contractors, or former employees of in-scope agencies to attend and answer questions, subject to protections under Head 35 and Article 38.1 of the Constitution.

#### Head 24: Offence of obstruction
Wilful obstruction, non-compliance, or provision of false/misleading information is an offence punishable on summary conviction by Class A fine or imprisonment up to 12 months, or both.

#### Head 25: Referral of individual misconduct
Where individual misconduct is identified during systemic work, Commission shall refer to Fiosrú, Judicial Council, LSRA, DPP, or other appropriate body. Commission shall not adjudicate individual liability. Mandatory referral SLAs to be set by regulation.

---

### PART 6: INTEGRITY DIRECTIVES

#### Head 26: Issuance of directives
(1) Where systemic integrity risk identified, Commission may issue binding Integrity Directive requiring amendment of policies, procedures, or practices within specified timeframe.  
(2) Directive must specify risk, required remedial action, and compliance deadline.  
(3) Agency must acknowledge receipt within 5 working days and submit compliance plan within 20 working days.

#### Head 27: Compliance monitoring
Commission monitors compliance and may publish non-compliance reports. Persistent non-compliance may be reported to Oireachtas committee and C&AG.

#### Head 28: Judicial review
In-scope agency may apply to High Court for judicial review within 28 days on grounds of ultra vires, procedural unfairness, or unreasonableness. No stay of directive unless court orders otherwise.

---

### PART 7: REPORTING AND ACCOUNTABILITY

#### Head 29: Annual report
Annual Report on State of Criminal Justice Integrity published within 6 months of financial year-end. Must include systemic findings, audit summaries, data analytics, compliance assessments, and recommendations.

#### Head 30: Special reports
Commission may publish special reports on urgent/significant matters at any time.

#### Head 31: Oireachtas accountability
Chairperson and CEO shall appear before Oireachtas Joint Committee on Justice upon request to present reports and discuss priorities. Commission accountable to Oireachtas, not to Minister.

#### Head 32: International compliance reporting
Commission compiles and submits justice-sector data for EU Anti-Corruption Directive and GRECO frameworks, coordinating with DoJ and DPENDR.

---

### PART 8: FINANCIAL PROVISIONS

#### Head 33: Grants and separate Vote
Annual grant paid from Central Fund as separate Vote distinct from Department of Justice Vote. Amount sanctioned by Minister for Public Expenditure, NDP Delivery and Reform.  
*Note: Critical for operational independence.*

#### Head 34: Accounts and audit
Commission keeps proper accounts submitted annually to Comptroller and Auditor General for audit. Accounts laid before Oireachtas with C&AG report.

---

### PART 9: MISCELLANEOUS

#### Head 35: Protection of disclosures
Nothing diminishes Protected Disclosures Act 2014 protections. Commission maintains secure, confidential whistleblower intake channels. Retaliation against disclosers cooperating with Commission is an offence.

#### Head 36: Confidentiality and data protection
All personal data processed per Data Protection Act 2018 and GDPR. Technical and organisational security measures mandatory. Dedicated DPO required. Prior consultation with DPC completed before high-risk processing commences.

#### Head 37: Regulations
Minister may make regulations giving effect to Act, including data schema standards, referral SLAs, and committee procedures. Regulations subject to positive Oireachtas resolution.

#### Head 38: Amendments to other enactments
Consequential amendments to Garda Síochána Act 2005, Prisons Act 2007, Courts Service Act 1998, Probation Service Act 2024, and Protected Disclosures Act 2014 to insert statutory cooperation obligations with CJOC. Set out in Schedule 3.
